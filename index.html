About This Journey

This project examines flight price patterns between Vancouver and Toronto through an interactive, time-based narrative. By breaking prices down by year, month, week, day, and departure time, the visualization helps uncover when flights are most affordable and how timing influences cost.

Home in Vancouver → Study in Toronto

According to Statistics Canada, 1.3% to 1.93% of bachelor’s degree graduates of post-secondary institutions in Ontario from 2012 to 2021 were from British Columbia. This group partially represents students whose home is in Vancouver but study in Toronto.

For these students, air travel often becomes a recurring part of their academic life. Flights may be necessary to return home during academic breaks, holidays, family events, or unexpected situations. Understanding how prices fluctuate across time can meaningfully affect travel planning and budgeting.

Overview of Price Fluctuations

Looking across an entire year of travel, flight prices rise and fall on a daily basis. This visualization captures those shifts from November 2025 to October 2026, showing the range between the cheapest and most expensive tickets students might encounter.

Looking across the year, prices are most unpredictable at the end of 2025, then become much steadier throughout 2026. In December 2025, the highest-price line shoots up with extreme spikes (including a peak around $2,586). In 2026, prices follow a more seasonal pattern: summer sits higher, while fall (especially October) becomes cheaper again.

Which Months Are Cheapest to Fly?

This monthly boxplot summarizes the daily lowest ticket prices for Vancouver → Toronto by month. Each box shows the typical price range (middle 50% of days), the black line marks the median, and the whiskers show unusually cheap or expensive days—so you can compare both cost and volatility.

The cheapest months to fly are January–April, while the most expensive (and least predictable) are November–December. Summer trends higher overall, and October offers deals if you catch the low days.

Do Flight Prices Increase Toward the End of the Month?

This chart zooms in on a smaller question: does timing within a month matter? We grouped flights into Week 1 to Week 5 and calculated the average cheapest price and average most expensive price for each week.

There isn’t a strong “end of month = always more expensive” pattern. Cheapest-price averages stay fairly consistent across weeks, but the most-expensive prices spike in Week 3—suggesting mid-month is where the risk of paying a premium is highest. Week 5 isn’t the most expensive, which challenges the assumption that prices steadily rise toward month-end.

Which Days Are Cheapest to Fly?

This heatmap breaks the year into a simple question: which day of the week tends to be cheaper to fly? Each cell shows the average ticket price for a specific month + weekday combination. Lighter cells mean cheaper fares, darker cells mean pricier ones—so you can quickly spot “good days” and seasonal shifts.

Overall, mid-week is usually the best bet for cheaper flights. Tuesday is often the cheapest day (with Wednesday close), while weekends tend to be riskier. When looking at the “most expensive” view, the spikes are more common on Mon/Sun, so those are the days to be extra careful with if your schedule is flexible.

When Is the Best Time of Day to Fly?

This scatterplot compares the cheapest and most expensive ticket prices across different departure times. Each point represents a flight, showing how price risk shifts throughout the day.

Early morning flights tend to cluster at higher prices, while later times can include more budget options— but also bigger spikes depending on demand.

Summary & Insights

Overall, our visualizations show that flight prices from Vancouver to Toronto don’t change smoothly — they swing based on when you travel. For students, that matters because trips are usually tied to fixed milestones (final exams, semester breaks, co-op start dates, family events), so the “wrong” timing can quickly blow a budget.

Once the month is chosen, the smaller decisions become “risk management.” Week-by-week, the cheapest averages don’t shift dramatically, but premium prices spike more in certain weeks. The day and time-of-day views reinforce that idea: weekday patterns shift by season, and certain times cluster with pricier tickets. For students, the practical takeaway is: lock in the right season first, then stay flexible on day/time to avoid premium-price traps.
